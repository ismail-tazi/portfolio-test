The challenge

An industrial manufacturer needed visibility across its entire supply chain — from raw-material procurement, through production, to warehousing and final distribution — but each stage lived in a separate system with no shared view, making it impossible to see where a delay in getting product to customers actually originated.

The approach

  • Modeled the full supply chain in SQL as a galaxy schema — four fact tables (fact_procurement, fact_production, fact_inventory, fact_shipments) sharing common dimensions (dim_date, dim_product, dim_plant, dim_supplier) so every stage could be compared on the same terms.
  • Loaded and validated data from each stage, resolving mismatched product and date keys across the four source systems.
  • Connected the model to Power BI and wrote DAX measures for lead time, on-time delivery, and inventory turnover at each stage.
  • Built a supply-chain-manager-facing dashboard with drill-through from an end-to-end view down to a single supplier, plant, or shipment.

The result

The dashboard traced a recurring delivery delay back to its actual source — supplier lead times on one raw-material category — instead of the distribution team being blamed for a bottleneck that started three stages upstream.

  • Galaxy schema linking 4 fact tables across the supply chain
  • DAX-driven lead time and inventory turnover at every stage
  • Drill-through from end-to-end view to a single supplier or shipment

What I'd do differently

Modeling four connected fact tables was a big step up from a single star schema — the hardest part was making sure dim_product and dim_date meant exactly the same thing in every source system, since one mismatched key silently breaks the whole chain. I'd build a shared dimension-key validation step earlier next time, before connecting anything to Power BI.
